Case Study: How I Built a Contractor's Local SEO System from Scratch

By Tony Beal  ·  April 2026  ·  Local SEO, Contractor Marketing, Web Systems

Most contractor websites are brochures. They list services, show a phone number, and sit there waiting for someone who already knows the company to look them up. That's not a marketing system — it's a business card online.

This is the story of how I built a real local SEO machine for All-Pro Metro East Construction — a 23-year-old owner-operated contractor serving the Metro East Illinois market. Starting from a basic site with no city-level coverage, we built a system that now has 400+ pages indexed by Google across 30+ cities and 13 service categories.

The Starting Point

Bill Sessions has been running All-Pro Metro East Construction for over two decades. Real work, real photos, real reviews, zero fluff. The business was built entirely on referrals and word of mouth — which is common for contractors of his tenure and quality. But referrals plateau. You can only grow as fast as your network grows.

The website he had was adequate but passive. It covered services at a high level, had a contact form, and showed some photos. What it didn't have was any city-level visibility — meaning when someone in Edwardsville typed "deck builder near me" or "concrete contractor Collinsville IL," the site wasn't showing up. At all.

That's the core problem with most contractor sites: they exist, but they don't rank. And they don't rank because Google needs geographic specificity to serve local results. A generic page that says "we serve Metro East Illinois" doesn't compete with a page that says "deck builder in Edwardsville IL — projects, photos, and reviews."

The Strategy: City × Service Coverage

Local SEO for contractors works on a simple matrix: city × service type. For every city you want to rank in, you need a dedicated page for each service you offer. A page for "decks in Collinsville IL." A page for "fencing in O'Fallon IL." A page for "concrete contractor in Edwardsville IL."

This isn't about stuffing keywords onto a generic page — it's about building genuinely useful, location-specific content that gives Google enough context to serve it to the right searcher.

For All-Pro, the service matrix was:

  • Decks, patios, concrete, fencing, landscaping
  • Bathroom remodel, kitchen remodel
  • Tree service, mulch & rock, landscape cleanup
  • Outdoor living, composite decking
  • City-specific deck builder pages (the highest-intent searches)

Across 30+ cities — Belleville, Collinsville, O'Fallon, Edwardsville, Fairview Heights, Mascoutah, Shiloh, Swansea, Waterloo, Highland, Columbia, and 20+ more — that's a large content matrix. The question is how to build it systematically without producing generic garbage that Google penalizes.

The Build: What We Actually Created

1. City Hub Pages

For each major city in the service area, we built a full hub page — not a thin keyword-stuffed placeholder, but a real page that covers all services Bill offers in that city, with local context, trust signals, and a clear path to contact. These pages include LocalBusiness schema, breadcrumb schema, FAQ schema with genuine questions homeowners ask, and review aggregates.

The city pages also include actual local context — neighborhoods, landmarks, and notes relevant to that specific city. Google rewards geographic authenticity. A page that mentions the specific neighborhoods in Edwardsville where Bill works ranks better than a generic city-name page.

2. Service × City Pages

For the highest-volume service categories, every city got its own page. Decks in Collinsville. Concrete in Mascoutah. Fencing in Shiloh. Each page built on a consistent template that covered: service overview, why it matters for that city's homes, what to expect from the process, pricing context, and a direct CTA to get an estimate.

The consistent structure matters because it lets Google understand the content category quickly, while the city-specific elements (neighborhood references, local examples) signal genuine geographic relevance.

3. Specialty Service Pages

Beyond the city matrix, we built standalone service authority pages — best fence companies in Metro East, composite decking options, outdoor living overview — that target higher-funnel searches (people researching before they're ready to call). These pages build topical authority and feed into the city-level pages through internal linking.

4. Blog Content

Ten blog posts targeting question-based searches: "composite vs. wood decking in Metro East," "how to choose a patio contractor," "concrete driveway maintenance in Illinois," "privacy fence ideas for O'Fallon." Each post ends with a hard CTA box linking to the relevant service page. Blog content builds authority for the topical cluster and captures the "research phase" searcher before they've made a decision.

5. Schema and Technical SEO

Every page got proper structured data: GeneralContractor LocalBusiness schema, FAQPage schema on service pages, BreadcrumbList, AggregateRating with real review data. The sitemap was built and submitted with proper priority weighting — city pages at 0.9, blog at 0.7, homepage at 1.0. Open Graph and Twitter card tags on all pages for social sharing. Preload hints on LCP images for page speed. No broken links, no dead redirects, clean robots.txt.

"Most contractor sites lose on technical basics, not content. Clean schema, fast load times, and a properly submitted sitemap give you a structural advantage before you've written a single word of content."

The Results

400+ pages now indexed by Google. Coverage across 30+ Metro East cities. Every major service category represented in every major city in the service territory.

More importantly, the architecture is built to compound. As Google indexes the new pages and assigns authority, the existing pages get stronger from internal linking. New blog posts reinforce the topical clusters. The city pages rank for long-tail searches that don't require domain authority to win — "deck repair Mascoutah IL" is not a competitive keyword nationally, but it's exactly what a homeowner in Mascoutah types when they're ready to hire someone.

That's the leverage of local SEO for contractors: the searches are low competition, high intent, and geographically specific. If you show up when someone types "fence company near me + city name," you're talking to someone who is ready to call.

What This Means for Other Contractors

If you're a contractor relying entirely on referrals and word of mouth, you're growing as fast as your network — and no faster. Local SEO is the multiplier that lets you capture demand you never knew existed.

The system I built for All-Pro is replicable. The architecture is consistent. The page templates are built. What changes is the city list, the service list, and the local context. A roofing company in St. Louis, a landscaper in Nashville, a remodeling contractor in Denver — the approach is the same.

What it requires:

  • A defined service area — which cities do you want to rank in?
  • A clear service list — what are the 5–10 services that drive your revenue?
  • Real business content — photos, reviews, and genuine local context (not AI filler)
  • Technical fundamentals — schema, sitemap, page speed, clean internal linking
  • Time — Google doesn't index and rank overnight. 60–90 days to start seeing meaningful movement.

The contractors who are getting leads from Google while their competitors get none aren't doing something magical. They have pages that match what homeowners are searching. That's the whole game.
