What Is Revenue Operations (RevOps)? A Plain-English Breakdown

By Tony Beal · April 28, 2026 · 8 min read · RevOps Sales Operations B2B

If you've seen "Revenue Operations" on a job posting or heard it in a meeting and thought "wait, what actually is that?" — you're not alone. It's one of those terms that got hot fast and is now used to mean three different things depending on who's talking.

I've spent 15 years in B2B sales and operations across manufacturing, pharma, and industrial markets. Here's the clearest breakdown I can give you.

The One-Sentence Definition

Revenue operations is the function that aligns sales, marketing, and customer success under shared systems, shared data, and shared process — so revenue is more predictable and less dependent on heroics.

That's it. Everything else is implementation detail.

Why RevOps Exists

Before RevOps became a formal function, most B2B companies ran three separate silos:

  • Marketing owned leads and top-of-funnel. They measured MQLs.
  • Sales owned pipeline and closes. They measured quota attainment.
  • Customer success owned retention and expansion. They measured churn and NPS.

The problem: each team had their own CRM views, their own definitions, their own data. A lead could be "qualified" in marketing and completely cold in sales. A closed deal could have terrible implementation notes that made CS onboarding a disaster. Revenue fell through the gaps between the teams — not because people were bad at their jobs, but because the systems weren't connected.

Revenue operations was created to close those gaps. Not by merging the teams, but by building the infrastructure that makes them work together without friction.

What RevOps People Actually Do

The day-to-day of a Revenue Operations role looks something like this:

1. CRM Architecture and Data Hygiene

You own the CRM — HubSpot, Salesforce, or whatever your stack is. You design the pipeline stages, define the deal fields, set up automation rules, and make sure the data in there is actually usable. Bad CRM data is one of the most common reasons sales teams miss quota while thinking they have a full pipeline.

2. Pipeline Management and Forecasting

You build the dashboards and reporting that tell leadership where revenue actually stands — not the optimistic number a sales rep typed in, but the weighted, stage-validated number that reflects reality. You run pipeline reviews, flag stalled deals, and keep the forecast honest.

3. Process Design and Handoff Workflows

When marketing passes a lead to sales, what happens? Who owns it, when, and what information travels with it? When sales closes a deal, what does customer success receive? RevOps designs those handoffs so nothing falls through the cracks.

4. Systems and Tool Management

The average B2B company has 10–15 sales and marketing tools. RevOps owns the stack, manages integrations, and makes sure data flows cleanly between systems. This is underrated work — a broken Zapier integration can cost you thousands of leads a month.

5. Enablement and Reporting

RevOps builds the reports that let reps know how they're performing, where to focus, and which accounts are worth pursuing. Not busywork dashboards — tools people actually use to make decisions in the field.

RevOps vs. Sales Operations — What's the Difference?

Sales ops is focused on the sales team specifically. RevOps is the broader function that connects sales with marketing and customer success.

Think of it this way: Sales ops runs the engine in the car. RevOps designs the whole drivetrain — engine, transmission, and wheels — so they all turn together.

In smaller companies, these are often the same role. In larger companies, they're separate functions that report to a Chief Revenue Officer.

What Makes a Great RevOps Person

The best RevOps operators I've seen (and the lens through which I'd describe my own career) have three things in common:

  1. They understand how revenue actually gets created — not just in theory, but from working the phones, managing accounts, or running deals themselves. Process design by people who've never sold is usually useless.
  2. They're comfortable in data — not necessarily data scientists, but they can build a pivot table, write a SQL query, or set up a CRM workflow without asking IT for help.
  3. They're obsessed with reducing friction — every unnecessary click, every unclear handoff, every report nobody reads is a tax on the revenue team. Great RevOps people hunt friction the way good engineers hunt technical debt.

RevOps in 2026: AI Is Changing the Role

The RevOps role is evolving fast right now. AI tools — for outreach personalization, lead scoring, pipeline forecasting, and CRM automation — are compressing what used to take a team of four into what one sharp operator can build and run solo.

I've spent the last two years building AI-powered outreach systems, deploying 40,000+ engineered prompts across manufacturing and pharma accounts, and running the full pipeline operation for a business with 3,700+ target accounts — as one person.

That's not a flex. It's a data point about where the role is going. The RevOps managers who learn to build AI systems on top of the traditional CRM/process work are going to be genuinely hard to replace.

Is RevOps Right for Your Business?

If your company has more than one person touching the sales process — and especially if you have separate marketing and customer success functions — you need someone thinking about revenue operations. Whether that's a dedicated hire, a fractional operator, or just getting your existing ops person to own the full funnel doesn't matter. What matters is that someone owns the system.

Without it, you're leaving money in the gaps between teams.
