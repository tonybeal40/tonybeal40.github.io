Section 1

The Targeting System

Most outreach breaks before the first message. The list is too broad, the follow-up is inconsistent, and the team has no shared process. Here is the framework I use to make targeting practical.

1

Define Your ICP with Precision

Do not start with job title alone. Start with company fit: industry, team size, buying complexity, and whether the business is showing signs that follow-up and sales operations actually matter right now.

Tool: practical company filters
2

Layer in Trigger Events

Cold outreach without a reason is noise. I look for changes that create urgency: a new leader, a hiring push, a launch, a new market move, or signs that the team is outgrowing its current process.

Tool: trigger-based account review
3

Cap Your Daily Volume

Cap daily volume so quality stays real. I would rather send a smaller number of messages with actual context than flood a list with forgettable outreach. More volume usually means less signal.

Rule: quality cap before quantity
4

Score Before You Touch

Run every account through a simple score so the team is not guessing. Fit, timing, and operational signal are enough to prioritize quickly and avoid wasting effort on low-likelihood outreach.

Rule: score before sequence

Section 2

The AI Prompt Stack

These prompts are useful because they create cleaner drafts faster. They are there to support judgment, not replace it.

Prompt 1 — Cold Email Opener
Write a 3-sentence cold email opener for [First Name] at [Company Name]. They recently [trigger event: e.g., "were promoted to VP of Sales" / "closed a Series B round" / "posted 6 open SDR roles"]. The email should reference a specific pain point related to [their situation] and position me as someone who solves it. Sound direct and human — no corporate filler, no "I hope this finds you well." End with a single soft CTA.
Prompt 2 — Subject Line Generator
Give me 7 subject line options for a cold email to a [job title] at a [company type] about [pain point]. Requirements: under 8 words each, no clickbait, no exclamation points, conversational tone. Include 2 that are questions, 2 that reference a specific outcome, and 3 that are curiosity-based.
Prompt 3 — Follow-Up Bump
Write a 2-sentence follow-up email to [First Name] who didn't reply to my first email about [topic]. Reference the original email briefly. Add one new data point or insight they haven't heard yet. No apologies for following up. End with a direct yes/no question.
Prompt 4 — Final Breakup Email
Write a "closing the loop" final email to [First Name]. Keep it to 3 sentences. Acknowledge they're likely busy. Leave the door open without being desperate. Make it easy for them to re-engage later with a single question. Tone: confident, no hard feelings.
Prompt 5 — LinkedIn Connection Note
Write a LinkedIn connection request note for [First Name] at [Company]. I sent them a cold email about [topic] on [Day 1]. The note should NOT pitch anything. It should be 2 sentences max: reference something specific about their work or company, and state why connecting makes sense. No "I'd love to connect" clichés.

Section 3

The 5-Touch Sequence

Most teams either under-follow up or overdo it. A simple cadence works better when every touch has a reason and the process is easy to repeat.

Day 1

AI-Personalized Cold Email

3 sentences max. Trigger-based opener, one pain point, one soft CTA ("Worth a 15-min call?"). Verified email only — never guess.

Day 3

LinkedIn Connection Request

No pitch. Reference something specific about their profile or company. Just connect and be human. Don't mention the email.

Day 5

Email Bump

2 sentences. Reference first email. Add one new data point — a stat, a case study, a relevant news item about their industry. Direct yes/no CTA.

Day 8

LinkedIn Engagement

Comment on a post they made. Add genuine value — don't just say "great post." Or send a voice note if you're connected. No pitch, just presence.

Day 14

Breakup Email

"Closing the loop" — confident, no desperation. Leave the door open. One final easy question. This email often gets the highest reply rate in the sequence.
