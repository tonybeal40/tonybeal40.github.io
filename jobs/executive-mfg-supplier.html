Executive Elevator Pitch — Confidential Client
Five ready-to-use responses for the scenarios you will face in this interview process. Each one is direct, specific, and designed to position you as the candidate who actually owns execution — not just manages it.
01 — “Tell me about yourself.”

I am an industrial B2B professional with 15+ years in technical account management and post-sale execution in custom manufacturing. My career has been in environments where someone has to own the execution after the sale closes — coordinating between customer requirements, overseas suppliers, and internal teams. At ProForm Manufacturing, I built a $6M+ program from zero in custom industrial manufacturing, owning NPI coordination, FAI/PPAP quality management, and supplier development across a casting, machining, and fabrication supply base. Before that, at Natoli Engineering, I managed OEM-level technical accounts where delivery against specification was non-negotiable. I am immediately available, 100% remote-capable, and looking for a role where I own outcomes.

02 — “Why this company?”

You’re doubling year over year and leadership is stepping back from execution to focus on growth. That means the post-sale execution gap is real, it’s growing, and whoever fills this role will have direct impact on whether that growth compounds or breaks. I’ve operated in exactly that environment — where the systems don’t yet exist and someone has to build them while simultaneously managing customer programs. That’s not a description of a challenge. It’s a description of where I do my best work.

03 — “You don’t come from traditional quality or supply chain. Why should we hire you?”

I come from execution. At ProForm, I wasn’t handed a quality system — I built one while managing OEM accounts under real pressure. The FAI documentation, the PPAP coordination, the corrective action process between customer requirements and supplier capabilities — that was mine to own. What differentiates me isn’t a specific degree or title. It’s the combination of technical fluency with customers, commercial instincts, and systems thinking. I can hold credible conversations with engineers in time zones 12 hours away and still close an upsell conversation on the same account.

04 — “What is your approach to supplier development?”

Start with visibility. You cannot improve what you cannot see. I build tracking systems first — delivery performance, quality escapes, corrective action status — before I start improving anything. Then I work with suppliers on root cause, not symptoms. The goal is always the same: reduce variance in delivery, quality, and communication so that customer programs don’t absorb supplier-side chaos. That applies whether the supplier is in Ohio or Shenzhen.

05 — “Where do you see the biggest opportunity here?”

Scalable execution infrastructure. A company doubling year over year has the revenue trajectory. The risk is that every new account adds manual coordination overhead. The opportunity is to build the program tracking, quality documentation, and supplier communication systems that make each new account easier to manage, not harder. I’ve done that from zero before. That’s the job.
